2020 BioLab — Meeting Talking Points

Google Ads disapproval → SEO / organic pivot · plus self-insured leads thread
Client: 20/20 BioLab (OneTest) Goal: scope the digital-growth engagement Stance: durable organic, not a paid workaround

1 Frame the problem honestly Open

Lead with the diagnosis so they trust the recommendation that follows.

  • Their ads are most likely disapproved under Google's Speculative & Experimental Medical Treatment policy — cancer-detection claims on a test that isn't FDA-approved.
  • Secondary triggers: health-in-personalized-advertising (cancer targeting/keywords), healthcare restrictions on DTC testing, and unsubstantiated claims (the sensitivity stats + fear framing).
  • First concrete step: read the exact disapproval reason in their Google Ads Policy Manager — fix the real thing, not a guess.
Say: “The ad problem isn't a setting we can toggle — it's structural to how Google treats non-FDA cancer claims. So the smart money is on building a channel we actually control.”

2 Set expectations: paid is structurally capped Reframe

  • As long as the ads make detection-efficacy claims for a non-FDA test, paid search stays restricted. This is policy, not a bug to patch.
  • That's why organic + content is the durable answer — it reaches the same audience without the paid gate.

3 E-E-A-T fix — and it does NOT need FDA approval Key point

Correct the likely misconception up front.

  • E-E-A-T is a content-credibility concept, not a regulatory one. FDA approval gates ads, not organic trust.
  • Build credibility on the signals they do have: CLIA/CAP lab accreditation, the peer-reviewed study (27K dataset, cited to PubMed), named credentialed team + advisory board, physician oversight, honest limitations.
  • Comparable: Galleri and most cancer blood tests are LDTs, not FDA-approved — they rank on E-E-A-T, not approval.
Say: “You don't need FDA approval to earn Google's trust organically — you need to show the lab accreditation, the published data, and real medical authorship. You already have those; they're just not surfaced.”

4 Education blog + keyword strategy Growth

  • Capture the informational queries paid can't serve: “what is multi-cancer early detection,” “cancer screening by age/risk,” per-cancer-type pages, “[competitor] vs” comparisons.
  • Topic-cluster hub, every post medically reviewed with a credentialed byline — the blog is how E-E-A-T scales.
  • Stay on screening/prevention/awareness framing; avoid symptom/diagnosis intent (misleading-content risk).
Set the clock: E-E-A-T fixes show in weeks; organic traffic compounds over 3–6+ months. It's an asset, not a faucet.

5 AI pre-publish compliance reviewer Differentiator

This is the part that sets the offer apart — and it's reusable IP.

  • An AI linter that runs before the human medical reviewer: flags uncited claims, prohibited language (cure/prevent/diagnose/guarantee), missing disclaimers, fear-based tone.
  • It's a pre-screen, not an approver — a credentialed human still signs off (required for E-E-A-T and liability).
  • Scales the content pipeline and keeps every post defensible against FTC/Google scrutiny.

6 If they still want paid — the legitimate path only Protect them

  • Advertise content / brand / wellness, not efficacy claims; or advertise a telehealth/physician service.
  • Manual appeal with documentation (CLIA/CAP + published study). Try other channels (Meta, native, email, affiliate).
If they raise “a company that bypassed it”: if that meant cloaking (showing reviewers a clean page, swapping landing pages, burner accounts), advise against it. Permanent Google bans when caught — but the real exposure is the FTC on unsubstantiated cancer claims, plus FDA/LDT and state AGs. Not worth it for a cancer product, and not something to put your name on.
